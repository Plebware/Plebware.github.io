# 🎬 The 10 Commandments of Great Video Editing

Great video editing is invisible. When done well, the audience doesn't notice the cuts, the transitions, or the colour correction – they only feel the story.

These ten principles apply whether you're editing a feature film, a YouTube tutorial, or a short social media clip. They are **evergreen** – they work regardless of software, genre, or skill level.

---

## 1. ✂️ Cut for the story, not the timeline

**The story comes first. Everything else serves it.**

It's easy to become attached to a beautiful shot, a clever transition, or a perfectly timed effect. But if it doesn't serve the story, it doesn't belong in the final cut.

Before making any edit, ask yourself: *"Does this move the story forward? Does it help the audience understand, feel, or care about what's happening?"*

If the answer is no, cut it.

### Practical tip:

- **Start with a paper edit.** Review your raw footage and note the key moments. Build your edit on paper (or in a spreadsheet) before touching the timeline. This keeps you focused on the story, not the software.
- **When in doubt, cut it out.** If a scene feels slow, it probably is. Be ruthless. Your audience will thank you.

---

## 2. 🗑️ Remove dead space

**Pacing is everything. Dead space kills momentum.**

Dead space is anything that doesn't contribute to the story: long pauses, unnecessary reactions, or footage that simply lingers too long.

Viewers have become accustomed to fast-paced content – especially online. A well-placed pause creates tension or emphasis, but an unintentional gap creates boredom.

### Practical tip:

- **After your first rough cut, watch it back and look for dead space.** Trim the start and end of each clip to remove unnecessary frames.
- **Don't be afraid to add a cutaway or b-roll** if a scene feels too slow. A brief visual distraction can maintain momentum while preserving essential dialogue or narration.

---

## 3. 🔄 Use transitions sparingly

**The best transitions are the ones you don't notice.**

Fancy transitions – wipes, zooms, and spin effects – are tempting, especially when you're learning new software. But overusing them distracts the audience and makes your edit feel amateurish.

The default cut is the most powerful transition you have. Use it 90% of the time.

### When to use transitions:

- A **dissolve** can indicate a passage of time or a change of location.
- A **jump cut** can create energy and urgency (but use with care).
- A **wipe** or **zoom** may be appropriate for specific stylistic choices (e.g., a retro video or a dramatic reveal).

### Practical tip:

- **Choose one or two transition styles for a project** and stick with them. Consistency is more professional than variety.
- **If you're using a transition, ask yourself:** *"Would a simple cut work better?"* Most of the time, the answer is yes.

---

## 4. 🔊 Keep audio consistent

**Audio is 50% of the viewing experience. Treat it accordingly.**

Viewers will tolerate a slightly grainy image, but they will not tolerate bad audio. Inconsistent volume, background noise, or distorted sound is jarring and amateurish.

### Key audio principles:

- **Dialogue should be clear and consistent.** Use compression and EQ to ensure speech is audible and free from background noise.
- **Music and sound effects should support the visuals**, not compete with them.
- **Room tone** (the ambient sound of a room) should match across cuts. A sudden change in background hum is distracting.

### Practical tip:

- **Set a consistent level for dialogue** – around -12dB to -6dB is a good starting point.
- **Use crossfades (audio dissolves)** between audio clips to avoid abrupt changes in background noise.
- **Check your audio in the final export** on different devices (laptop speakers, headphones, phone). What sounds good in your editing suite may not translate to other environments.

---

## 5. 🎨 Don't overuse effects

**Effects should enhance, not overwhelm.**

Colour filters, lens flares, glitch effects, and other visual treatments can be powerful tools when used intentionally. But they become a crutch when used to cover up weak storytelling or poor footage.

### When to use effects:

- **If the effect supports the story or emotion** – e.g., a desaturated look for a somber scene, or a glitch effect for a digital theme.
- **If the effect is consistent** – don't suddenly introduce a dramatic effect in the middle of a cleanly edited video.

### Practical tip:

- **Apply effects sparingly and with purpose.** If you can't justify the effect, leave it out.
- **Use effects as a final polish**, not as a foundation. Your edit should work without them.

---

## 6. 🎨 Color correction before color grading

**Correction fixes reality. Grading stylises it.**

Many editors jump straight to creative colour grading without first fixing the technical issues. This is a mistake.

### Color correction:
- Balances white balance
- Adjusts exposure and contrast
- Ensures consistency between clips
- Neutralises colour casts

### Color grading:
- Creates a mood or look
- Adds stylistic choices
- Applies LUTs (Look-Up Tables) for a specific aesthetic

### Practical tip:

- **Correct, then grade.** Always fix the technical issues before applying creative choices.
- **Use scopes, not your eyes.** Your monitor lies – scopes (waveform, RGB parade, vectorscope) give you objective data.
- **Check skin tones.** They should look natural in your corrected footage.

---

## 7. 🔗 Maintain visual continuity

**The viewer should never feel lost.**

Continuity is about maintaining a consistent visual world. It includes:

- **180° rule** – keep the camera on the same side of the action so the viewer doesn't get disoriented.
- **Match cuts** – action should flow naturally from one shot to the next.
- **Eyeline** – actors should look at the correct eyeline in reverse shots.
- **Props and wardrobe** – continuity mistakes in props or clothing are jarring.

### Practical tip:

- **Review your edit for continuity errors.** Watch it back with a critical eye.
- **If you can't fix a continuity error**, consider using a cutaway or b-roll to distract the viewer.

---

## 8. 👥 Edit for your audience

**The audience determines the edit.**

A YouTube tutorial requires a different rhythm than a music video. A documentary needs a different approach than a social media ad. Know your audience and edit accordingly.

### Key questions to ask:

- **What is the expected duration?** A 10-minute video needs a different structure than a 60-second clip.
- **What is the viewing environment?** Phone viewers watch differently than cinema audiences.
- **What is the emotional goal?** Should the audience laugh, cry, learn, or be inspired?

### Practical tip:

- **Watch content in your niche.** Pay attention to pacing, structure, and style. Borrow techniques that work, adapt those that don't.
- **Test your edit with a sample audience.** Get feedback before finalising.

---

## 9. 📤 Export using the correct settings

**The best edit is ruined by a poor export.**

Export settings are not a one-size-fits-all. The correct settings depend on where the video will be viewed.

### Key export considerations:

- **Platform:** YouTube, Vimeo, and other platforms have recommended settings.
- **Resolution:** 1080p (Full HD) or 4K for most platforms.
- **Frame rate:** Match the frame rate of your source footage (24fps for film, 30fps or 60fps for online video).
- **Codec:** H.264 or H.265 for most online platforms.
- **Bitrate:** Higher bitrate means better quality, but larger file sizes. Aim for 15-20 Mbps for 1080p, 40-50 Mbps for 4K.

### Practical tip:

- **Export to an intermediate format** (e.g., ProRes or DNxHR) if you need to send the project to someone else.
- **For YouTube, follow their recommended settings** – they'll give you the best balance of quality and file size.

---

## 10. 💾 Keep the original footage

**Your raw footage is your insurance policy.**

Storage is cheap. Regret is expensive. Never delete your original footage, even after you've exported the final version.

### Why keep originals:

- **You may need to re-export** for a different platform or format.
- **You may want to revisit the project** years later (director's cut?).
- **You may have missed something** that becomes important in a future edit.

### Practical tip:

- **Organise your files.** Create a project folder with sub-folders for raw footage, audio, graphics, and exports.
- **Back up your footage.** Use an external drive or cloud storage.
- **Keep a project archive.** Even after the project is complete, store the project file and assets.

---

## 🎯 Final Thought

Great editing is not about fancy effects or complex transitions. It's about serving the story, respecting the audience, and making every second count.

These ten commandments are not rules to follow blindly. They are principles to return to when you're stuck, uncertain, or tempted to overcomplicate your edit.
